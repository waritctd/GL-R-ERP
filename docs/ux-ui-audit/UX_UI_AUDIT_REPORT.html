1. Executive summary

GL&R-ERP is a Thai-language HR + Sales/CRM portal. This audit drove the mock frontend as all seven seeded personas across 16 routes, desktop & mobile, capturing 141 screenshots.

Overall verdict — 3 / 5 (Usable with significant friction, strong in parts). The HR core is genuinely well-built: guided forms with accessible inline validation, consistent ConfirmDialog usage, skeletons, toasts, and a polished employee/leave/overtime experience. The newer Sales/CRM stack is a tier behind — powerful and information-rich, but with a mobile layout bug on its most-used screen, toast-only form validation, un-tokenized styling, and a route-guard gap. Mobile is the weakest dimension overall.

Biggest barriers to production readiness

  • Destructive actions without confirmation (UX-02) — HR approves/rejects profile changes with one click, no undo.
  • Inconsistent, less-accessible sales-form validation (UX-03) — toast-only errors on long deal forms.
  • Front-end route-guard gap (UX-19) — /ceo-settings loads for any authenticated role by URL.
  • Deal-flow orchestration & trust (UX-31, UX-32) — driving the pipeline end-to-end (see §5b) showed the awaited role action is rarely the prominent CTA, and status badges can contradict the underlying document/state.

Strongest parts

  • HR self-service (leave/overtime/profile) — accessible inline validation, quota context, confirm dialogs.
  • The sales deal list — phase cards, lifecycle/flag filters, progress bars, per-row status: excellent information design.
  • Shared primitives — Modal focus traps, aria-live toasts, skeleton loaders, notification bell.
Immediate decision: ship the HR core with confidence after fixing UX-02 and UX-06; treat the Sales/CRM stack as "beta" until UX-34/UX-03 land.

2. Product & user context

Purpose. GL&R-ERP is a GL&R internal portal combining an HR core (employees, attendance, leave, overtime, payroll, profile self-service) with a Sales/CRM stack (deal pipeline / price-requests, quotations, deposit notices, commissions, FX & pricing config, product catalog). It is explicitly "not a complete ERP yet" and is on a Tailwind-first migration off a legacy global stylesheet.

Assumed environment. Thai-language desktop-first internal tool; staff on office desktops for admin-heavy work (payroll, attendance, pricing) and phones for lighter, on-the-go tasks (leave, checking a deal). Expected personality: trustworthy, efficient, operational.

5b. Sales deal-flow walkthrough

This pass drove the pipeline fully end-to-end across roles on one persistent mock session (its in-memory DB survives role switches without reload): Sales submits → Import picks up, drafts the factory email & proposes price → CEO calculates (CIF), approves/rejects → Sales quotes → Account records the money receipt. 15 seed deals span all 14 stages (ids 1–15); each role's action was exercised on a deal genuinely in that state, and two real transitions were verified (Import propose → "รอการอนุมัติ"; CEO approve → "อนุมัติแล้ว").

The pipeline is genuinely comprehensive and well-modelled — 14 stages, phase tracker, structured lost-reasons, reason-required stage edits, clear approve/reject copy, auto-drafted factory emails, and good empty states. The flow completes. The friction is orchestration, trust & layout: UX-31 (the awaited role action is a below-the-fold panel, not the prominent CTA), UX-32 (status badge vs document/state mismatch), UX-33 (deal-flow form validation).

UX-04 Company dashboard shows HR headcount & approval stats to roles that have no path to act on them P2

Category: Navigation / Information architectureRoute: /Viewport: DesktopRole: ceoFrequency: AlwaysEffort: MConfidence: High
Observed
The shared EmployeeDashboard renders company HR StatCards (28 พนักงาน Active, 4 รออนุมัติทั้งหมด, OT/leave counts) for CEO, but CEO's sidebar has no Employees / HR overview / Requests / Payroll links, and those cards are non-navigable for CEO. The metric is a dead end — visible but not drillable.
User impact
CEO sees numbers that imply an action ('4 approvals') with no way to reach them, creating confusion and eroding trust in the dashboard's usefulness.
Root cause
One dashboard component is reused across all roles; stat visibility is not gated to the viewer's actual navigation rights.
UX principle
Match between system and real world; visibility of system status without dead ends
Recommendation
Either hide stats the role can't reach, or make each stat's drill-through available to that role. Prefer a role-scoped dashboard card set.
Acceptance criteria
  • Every stat shown on a role's dashboard is either actionable by that role or clearly labelled read-only.
  • No non-navigable 'pending' counts are shown to roles that cannot act.
Validation
Log in as CEO; confirm no dashboard stat implies an action CEO cannot reach.

Recommendation

10b. Visual design critique — unvarnished

§10 covers the design system structurally. This section is the subjective visual judgement, stated plainly. It is one reviewer's opinion, grounded in the 188 captures — not a measurement.

Verdict: competent, generic, and visibly built by two different teams. It reads as a well-executed admin template rather than a designed product. Nothing is ugly; almost nothing is memorable — strip the logo and it is hard to distinguish from any other Tailwind SaaS dashboard. For an internal ERP that is a defensible trade, but it should be called what it is: mostly defaults, not decisions. The sharper problem is that the HR half and the Sales half look like different products — HR screens are airy and tokenized, sales screens are cramped, chip-heavy and carry ~450 inline hex values. Users read that as "this part is finished, that part isn't."

What is genuinely good

  • The deal list is the best screen in the app, and it isn't close. Phase cards that double as filters, lifecycle chips with counts, per-row stage badge plus segmented progress, stale-day indicators. That is real information design and it earns its density.
  • Tables. Generous row height, restrained borders, status badges that actually read at a glance.
  • Empty states are properly built — icon, title, description, CTA — instead of a centred "No data".
  • Skeleton loaders exist, and the OT approve dialog states its exact consequence. Someone cared here.

What is mediocre

  • The dashboard is a wall of zeros. The CEO landing shows 28, 4, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 1, 13 — nine cards, five of them zero, all at equal visual weight, big bold numerals shouting nothing. A dashboard whose dominant glyph is "0" reads as a dead system, and it is the first screen every user sees.
  • The sidebar is fifteen items tall, each two lines (Thai label + English subtitle) — roughly thirty lines of navigation chrome. The English subtitle is dead weight for Thai staff who already read the Thai, and it doubles the scan cost of the most-used component in the product.
  • The palette is ~90 flat tokens with no scale — a bag of hex values rather than a system, with two competing tone vocabularies (indigo/teal/amber/rose vs success/warning/danger/info) in which the same green carries three names. No dark mode.

What is actually bad

  • The ticket detail is chip soup. Three rows, 14+ pills — payment 5, procurement 9, plus delivery counters — in three visual states (green+check = done, solid = current, plain = pending) that are too similar at that size. It is a status dump, not a status display: no entry point, no hierarchy. This is the screen sales staff live in all day.
  • The login page wastes half the screen — a 50% white void with a small logo top-left, while each role card misaligns its icon (flush left) against its label (centred). First impression, and it looks unfinished (UX-13, UX-14).
  • The over-payment checkbox in the money-receipt modal has its label pushed to the far right and wrapped across two lines, visually divorced from the box — on the screen where cash is confirmed.

The blind spot nobody has addressed: Thai typography

Type sizes are hard-coded in px (11/12/13/14/16/22…) with body line-height: 1.6 — Latin defaults applied to Thai. Thai stacks vowels above consonants and tone marks above those, plus below-vowels; at 11–13px with 1.6 leading those marks crowd and can clip. The app's secondary text — record meta lines, the English nav subtitles, table meta, helper text, chip labels — sits squarely in that band. Thai UI wants roughly 1.7–1.9 leading and a ~14px floor. Right now the densest, most-read text in a Thai-first product is set at Latin metrics. Filed as UX-36; it is the highest-leverage visual fix in the app and costs almost nothing.

Brand

There isn't one. Four coloured squares and a "GL&R HR" wordmark that is simply wrong on every sales and pricing screen (UX-21). Indigo #4f46e5 is the most default primary in the industry. Defensible for an internal tool — but that is absence-of-decision, not restraint.
